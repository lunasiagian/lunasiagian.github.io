On What Things Mean

BY LUNA TALITA SIAGIAN◆ JUN 2, 2026◆ 7 MIN READ

Why I read code and fiction with the same pair of eyes, and what gets lost in the translation between them.

CH.18 FIELD NOTES · NEW ERIDU REC

There is a habit I have never been able to break: I read everything as if it were trying to tell me something it could not say directly. A function name. A line of dialogue. The way someone ends a text message. I treat them all as surfaces with something underneath.

This is, I think, what it means to be a linguist who also writes code and fiction. The three are not separate careers. They are the same question asked in three grammars.

The same pair of eyes

When I read a program, I am not only asking what does this do. I am asking what the author believed about the world when they wrote it — what they thought was worth naming, what they left implicit, where they trusted the reader to fill in the gap. Code is a text. It has a voice. It has things it is embarrassed about and hides in a helper function three files away.

Meaning is never in the symbol. It is in the distance between the symbol and what it points at.

Fiction works the opposite way and arrives at the same place. A story withholds on purpose. It trusts the gap. The pleasure of reading is the pleasure of closing a distance the writer measured out for you in advance.

What gets lost

The hard part is the translation. When I try to move an idea from one grammar to another — from a feeling into a sentence, from a sentence into a system — something always leaks out at the seam. The leak is not failure. The leak is the evidence that the thing was real.

So this is what these field notes are for: to stand at the seam and describe what leaks. Through code, through fiction. Whichever grammar the week hands me.
